## Why Delano's Climate and Housing Stock Make Heat Pump Selection Matter

Delano sits at roughly 315 feet of elevation in Kern County, and its climate is classified as a hot semi-arid zone — long, dry summers with daytime highs regularly above 100°F and mild but genuine winters that dip into the low 30s on valley fog nights. That temperature range matters when sizing a heat pump: a unit spec'd only for moderate climates can lose efficiency below 35°F, which means homeowners near the Randolph Street corridor or out in West Delano's newer subdivisions need equipment with a rated heating capacity that holds up when overnight lows actually arrive.

The housing stock adds another layer. Retrofitting a ducted heat pump into one of these homes requires a duct load calculation and often duct sealing or replacement before the new system can perform as rated. Newer construction on the west side generally has better-insulated envelopes and existing duct systems sized for central air, making those properties more straightforward candidates for a direct heat pump swap.

## The Installation and Repair Process, Calibrated to Delano Conditions

Every heat pump job in Delano starts with a Manual J load calculation — no guessing on tonnage based on square footage alone. Valley homes gain heat through west-facing walls and low-pitched roofs in ways that a rule-of-thumb estimate misses. After load calc, the process follows a clear sequence:

1. **Site assessment** — existing ductwork condition, electrical panel capacity (heat pumps often require a 240V circuit upgrade), and outdoor unit placement relative to prevailing winds and sun exposure.
2. **Equipment selection** — matching SEER2 rating and heating capacity to Delano's climate data. For most residential applications here, a 16–18 SEER2 unit with a low-ambient heating rating is the practical sweet spot between upfront cost and long-term efficiency.
3. **Permit and inspection** — Kern County and the City of Delano require mechanical permits for HVAC replacements. We pull the permit, schedule the inspection, and hand you the signed-off paperwork.
4. **Installation and commissioning** — refrigerant line sets, electrical connections, thermostat wiring, and a full system startup check including airflow measurements at each register.
5. **Repair calls** — when an existing heat pump short-cycles, loses heating mode, or trips a breaker, diagnosis starts at the outdoor unit (capacitors, contactor, refrigerant charge) and works inward. Refrigerant recovery and recharge are federally regulated under EPA Section 608 — this is not a do-it-yourself job, regardless of who does the work.
