## Why Delano Homes Go Through Water Heaters Faster Than Average

Kern County groundwater hardness is a documented issue, and Delano's supply is no exception. Calcium and magnesium carbonate settle at the bottom of storage tanks, creating an insulating crust that makes your burner or heating element work overtime. The rumbling or popping you hear from an aging tank is often that sediment layer cracking under heat — a reliable sign the unit is in its final months. In the older housing stock along the Randolph Street corridor and the Cecil Avenue corridor, many homes still run original galvanized supply lines, which compound the problem by shedding rust particles that accelerate tank corrosion. ## Tankless vs. Tank: Choosing the Right System for Your Delano Property

The tankless-vs-tank question comes up on nearly every replacement call, and the honest answer depends on your household size, your gas supply, and your water quality situation.

**Storage tank units** (typically 40- or 50-gallon) cost less upfront, are straightforward to replace in homes already plumbed for them, and recover quickly enough for most families. If your current unit is a standard tank and your budget is the priority, a direct replacement is often the fastest path back to hot water.

**Tankless (on-demand) units** eliminate standby heat loss, deliver continuous hot water, and can last five to ten years longer than a tank — but they require adequate gas line capacity (often a larger dedicated line), proper venting, and in Delano's hard-water environment, a whole-house water softener or descaling system is strongly recommended to protect the heat exchanger. Without scale management, a tankless unit in this area can develop flow restrictions within a few years.
