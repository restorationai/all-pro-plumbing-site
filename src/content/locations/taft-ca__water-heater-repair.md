## Why Taft Properties See Water Heater Problems Early

The housing stock in Ford City and South Taft tells the story. Most of these workers' cottages were built between the 1920s and 1950s to house petroleum industry crews, and many still have their original galvanized steel supply lines. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out over time, and that rust and scale doesn't just slow your water pressure — it accelerates sediment accumulation inside the tank. When Taft's notoriously hard water runs through aging pipe into a tank that's never been flushed, the sediment layer at the bottom can reach an inch or more. You'll hear it: a low rumble or popping sound when the burner fires, the heater working harder and longer to push heat through that insulating layer of grit.

The dry, hot climate adds another layer of stress. Summer temperatures regularly push past 100°F in Taft, and thermal expansion cycles — the tank heating and cooling repeatedly through those extremes — wear out anode rods, pressure relief valves, and tank seams faster than in milder climates. A pressure relief valve that starts weeping isn't a minor annoyance; it's the tank telling you something is wrong with internal pressure.
